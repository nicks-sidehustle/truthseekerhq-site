Trump Orders Pentagon to Release UFO Files — What We Know

THE DIRECTIVE (February 19, 2026)

President Trump has ordered Pentagon Chief Pete Hegseth and other government agencies to begin the process of identifying and releasing classified files related to aliens, extraterrestrial life, UAPs (Unidentified Aerial Phenomena), and UFOs.

After decades of government secrecy, stonewalling, and "we can neither confirm nor deny" responses, the floodgates might finally be opening. But this disclosure didn't happen in a vacuum—it was triggered by a single podcast comment that went viral faster than the Pentagon could contain it.

The Comment That Changed Everything

It started on February 18th during former President Obama's appearance on "Pod Save America." When asked about UFOs, Obama casually dropped what might be the most significant admission in disclosure history:

"They're real, but I haven't seen them."

The comment was delivered with Obama's characteristic cool—no dramatic pause, no weight given to what he'd just said. But the internet exploded. Within hours, #ObamaUFO was trending globally. News outlets scrambled to fact-check. The Pentagon went into damage control mode.

Trump's Calculated Gamble

Trump's response was swift and strategic. On Truth Social, he accused Obama of leaking classified information: "Obama just admitted UFOs are real on live podcast. If this is classified intel, someone needs to answer for this breach."

But it was his Air Force One comments that revealed the real play. When reporters pressed him about UFOs, Trump said: "I don't know if they're real or not. But the American people deserve to know what their government knows. We're going to find out."

Translation: If Obama wants to play disclosure games, Trump will own the narrative. And if there's nothing there, he'll be the president who called their bluff.

What Files Could Actually Be Released?

The Pentagon has been releasing UFO-related materials in dribs and drabs since 2017. But Trump's directive could finally open the vault on decades of classified encounters. Here's what we know exists:

  • AATIP Files: The Advanced Aerospace Threat Identification Program ran from 2007-2012 with a $22 million budget. Only 3 videos have been released.
  • AARO Database: The All-domain Anomaly Resolution Office has catalogued over 800 unexplained incidents since 2021.
  • Historical Cases: Roswell, Phoenix Lights, USS Nimitz encounters—files that have been classified for decades.
  • Foreign Intelligence: UFO encounters reported by allied militaries, sharing agreements with other nations.
  • Scientific Analysis: Materials allegedly recovered from crash sites, biological samples, technological assessments.

Reality Check: What Will We Actually Get?

Here's what disclosure advocates need to understand: Presidential directives don't automatically equal alien revelations. The intelligence community has spent decades perfecting the art of compliance without disclosure.

Expect heavily redacted documents. Expect "we investigated and found no evidence of extraterrestrial origin." Expect mundane explanations for extraordinary encounters. The Pentagon has been preparing for this moment since the Navy UFO videos leaked in 2017.

"You can release every UFO file and still reveal nothing. Redaction is an art form, and the Pentagon are master artists."

The Congressional Factor

This doesn't exist in a political vacuum. Congress held the first UFO hearings in over 50 years in 2022. Senators Marco Rubio and Kirsten Gillibrand have been pushing for more transparency. The momentum was already building—Obama's comment just lit the fuse.

But there's also the national security angle. If the U.S. military has been tracking advanced craft with unknown origins for decades, admitting it publicly raises uncomfortable questions about our defense capabilities.

What Happens Next

The Pentagon now has 90 days to provide Trump with an assessment of what can be declassified "without compromising national security or ongoing investigations." Translation: they have 90 days to figure out how to give him something without giving him everything.

Watch for these developments:

  • Expedited release of previously announced UAP reports
  • Declassification of "low-risk" historical encounters
  • More Navy UFO videos from the "Tic-Tac" era
  • Scientific analysis of materials (heavily redacted)
  • Inter-agency coordination meetings (the real action happens here)

THE BOTTOM LINE

We're about to get the biggest UFO document dump in U.S. history. Whether it reveals aliens, advanced foreign technology, or just decades of bureaucratic incompetence remains to be seen. But for the first time since Roswell, the burden of proof has shifted from "prove they exist" to "prove they don't."
